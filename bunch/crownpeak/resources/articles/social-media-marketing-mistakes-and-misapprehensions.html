Social media marketing mistakes and misapprehensions

Speaking with the right tone in marketing content can be like walking a tightrope - too casual and organizations could develop a reputation as being unprofessional, too officious and they risk turning off prospective clients by being inaccessible. Perhaps nowhere is this balance more difficult to strike than on social media.

Social media has quickly become one of the most powerful channels for marketing messages. It is widely used and accessed almost everywhere, meaning organizations could reach potential clients almost anywhere they happen to be. Recent research has shown that up to 76 percent of mobile users access social networking sites like Facebook and Twitter on their devices. And with market research firm ComScore putting the number of U.S. smartphone subscribers at more than 101 million, this is an enormous segment of the population that can't be ignored.

With such a big potential payoff - and plenty of room for error - marketers need to take care when crafting their content for social media by avoiding some of the common pitfalls and "busting" the social media myths.

Social media content mistakes

Mark Twain is often quoted as having said, "I would like to have written a shorter letter but didn't have the time." Marketers crafting content for Facebook or Twitter might know exactly what he means. It can often be more difficult to write a concise, informative message than a longer, more detailed one. Social media needs to be given equal consideration as other forms of content - it can't be quickly tossed off in a few minutes.

After allocating the proper time in the schedule to composing social media content, there's still the challenge of figuring out what to say and how to say it. Organizations can't simply use the channel as another outlet for their sales material. According to social media marketer Fae Esperas, providing content that is too technical for the average consumer, too "salesy," or just plain boring is the surest way to misuse social media. Making content more personable, not deluging followers with messages, and adding exciting visuals are all ways to engage with consumers through social media, she said in a recent Business 2 Community article.

What social media is not

Freelance writer and content marketer Iliana Snow also recently took to Business 2 Community to enlighten fellow marketers about some of the most common misconceptions concerning social media marketing. She noted that, while it may not cost anything to sign up for social media sites, organizations will still need to invest in strategic planning, content generation, analysis and production, not to mention ads and other promotions - in other words, social media isn't free.

Neither is it a replacement for traditional forms of marketing or other web-based engagement strategies, she indicated. Social media campaigns have the potential to go viral, carry lower overall costs than other channels and have more easily measurable return on investment, but they are not a marketing silver bullet. A marketer must also continue to leverage other strategies, such as search engine optimization, to win eyes on the internet.

"As of now, the 'virtualization' of the masses is not a hundred percent complete and we can only make assumptions about whether it will ever be complete," she wrote. "[Search engine optimization], on the other hand, is important in getting you discovered on search engines. You can’t be discovered through social media alone. Google is still everyone’s friend - take advantage of all tools out there to get your message across and learn about the nuances of each."

Social media can be a powerful marketing tool if used correctly, but organizations can't underestimate the resources they'll need to use it effectively.
