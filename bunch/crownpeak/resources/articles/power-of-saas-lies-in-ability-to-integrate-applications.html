Power of SaaS lies in ability to integrate applications

The adoption of software as a service (SaaS), has risen markedly in recent years, changing the way many organizations operate. SaaS offerings address increasingly niche functions and organizations are choosing to use a range of applications rather than a single enterprise software suite, as they have in the past, according to a recent GigaOM column by integration expert Ross Mason.

He explained that, in this new computing environment, competitive advantage will lie in an organization's ability to integrate its siloed cloud applications rather than in the variety of functions any one piece of software can provide.

The effect of SaaS on enterprise systems

A recent Gartner report highlighted the degree to which SaaS use is on the rise globally. The consultancy's survey found that 71 percent of organizations have begun using SaaS since 2009. In the Asia-Pacific region, many organizations are using SaaS to access certain types of applications for the first time, while in the United States and Europe, Middle East and Africa (EMEA) regions, organizations are largely using SaaS to replace legacy systems.

For many organizations, SaaS is seen as a way to avoid expanding or replacing data center infrastructure, David Cappuccio, chief of research for the infrastructure teams with Gartner, told attendees at the consultancy's London Data Center Summit, according to ZDNet. He noted that analyses of many organizations' application portfolios found that just 15 to 25 percent of applications are business critical.

"If long term I move these applications or non-critical services off elsewhere, then suddenly the size of the data center and the amount of equipment I need to support those critical ones shrinks dramatically," Cappuccio said, according to ZDNet.

As organizations realize they can assign certain applications to the cloud, they are abandoning all-inclusive enterprise software suites for best-of-breed SaaS solutions designed for specific functions, Mason explained. However, while applications in traditional software systems are designed to work together, integrating cloud applications can be more difficult due to potentially large numbers of different solutions organizations might use.

Integration as a competitive advantage

SaaS allows organizations to pick and choose the best application for each business need rather than settling for acceptable applications within a closed software suite. As a result, any organization can now theoretically tap into the advantages of best-of-breed solutions for tasks such as customer relationship management (CRM) or enterprise resource planning (ERP).

However, these separate SaaS applications are not necessarily designed to communicate intuitively with each other, let alone with existing on-premise solutions that organizations want to continue using. While each application should have an API designed to share its data, organizations generally need to be able to sync information among multiple applications in real time, which can become complicated, Mason noted.

"Typically, integration needs to synchronize information between two or more applications, providing data transformation, security, reliability, visibility and error handling," he wrote. "Ideally this all happens in real-time so that your applications don’t get out of sync and users are always working with the most up-to-date information."

Organizations using tools such as cloud content management systems (CMS) that enable extensive integration will derive a greater benefit from SaaS tools and stand out from their competitors, Mason said.

"Competitive advantage is no longer in the applications you use, it’s in the platform you choose to connect them with," he wrote.

In a cloud-oriented computing environment where applications are standard for all users, organizations can look to create an advantage by adopting solutions that focus on bringing multiple types of SaaS together.
