Four characteristics of successful transnational branding

As organizations move into new international markets, they are faced with the dual challenges of maintaining their brand image while localizing their offering and message for new audiences. Achieving this balance may lie in the organization's ability to effectively "transbrand" itself, according to a recent Harvard Business Review blog post by marketing professor Dae Ryun Chang and visual communication professor Don Ryun Chang.

Transbranding, according to Chang and Chang, consists of "using brands to connect and effectively move between divergent markets," or bridging differences to create a consistent narrative. Organizations that effectively transbrand exhibit four main qualities:

  1. 1. Transitional: As the market changes, brands must also adapt - without losing loyal customers who might be alienated by a major shift. For instance, when a major coffee brand recently repositioned itself to offer more than coffee, its new logo maintained familiar visual elements to signal that core values remained intact.
  2. 2. Transcendent: Rather than targeting niche audiences with different angles, brands should look for an appealing, unique message that can cut across a wide range of markets. The video for the song "Gangnam Style" was able to become the most viewed YouTube video of all time despite being in Korean because its visual content was unmistakably likeable and funny, the authors noted as an example
  3. 3. Transformable: In a digital environment, branded content should take advantage of the medium to offer interactivity and even customization geared to specific users. By creating a dynamic experience with their content, organizations can increase their visibility in a way not possible with static media.
  4. 4. Transparent: Social media allows audiences to control perception of a brand as easily as paid marketers, so organizations need to be honest with their customers and clients while continuing to tell a unique story and reveal new surprises.

What about translation?

To meet each of these characteristics of transbranding, organizations need to think beyond a more familiar "trans" word - translation - and embrace a holistic globalization strategy that includes internationalization and localization as well. According to Bert Esselink's book "A Practical Guide to Localization," translation is just one component of localization, which includes adapting the messaging and formatting of content to fit local cultural norms and customs. Internationalization, in which design is crafted to allow for a wide range of character sets or reading behaviors, is a prerequisite to localization. When organizations are creating a website to reflect the principles of transbranding, they must consider the ways these branding elements go beyond translation.

"Language is the starting point," Don DePalma, founder of market research firm Common Sense Advisory, told FierceContentManagement.com in a recent e-book article. "We keep coming back to the dictum that 'People don’t buy what they can’t understand.' Our research consistently shows that people respond best when they’re addressed in their native language. But there’s more than just the right language - people need to feel that the materials 'speak' to them."

To create a universal brand, organizations should focus on how to communicate the qualities that make their brand transitional, transcendent, transformable and transparent across different audiences - a process that includes, but also goes beyond, translation.
