Tips for localizing a marketing message

Website and marketing content that works well in the United States can fall flat when it reaches overseas audiences, so organizations need to make appropriate adjustments when branching out into international markets. Organizations need to articulate a clear voice and message and maintain consistency across multiple websites, but they also need to localize their content to present the most relevant information to audiences, in the most effective vernacular. To strike this balance, organizations must consider what content can remain the same and what must change on sites for various countries. The following actions will help organizations make smart changes as they refine messaging for international sites: reconsider colloquial language, tweak B2B communications to reflect local priorities, and adopt a sophisticated approach to translation.

Swing and a miss

Pop culture references, colloquial phrases and sports analogies are all big guns in the marketing arsenal, but they are only effective if the audience gets the reference. For example, baseball might be America's Pastime, but there are only a handful of other countries where the sport is played. In a recent article for Fast Company, marketing expert David Lavenda mentioned a situation in which his marketing team replaced a sports reference with a quote from a French playwright during a campaign in that country.

Another consideration organizations need to make, particularly when engaging in business-to-business marketing and communication, is that not all countries espouse the same business values and ideals. Lavenda highlighted a study on the effects of workplace distractions and the way media outlets in different countries reported the news. While German and American newspapers focused on lost revenue and productivity due to distractions, press in Britain led with the rude behavior of distracted workers.

Managing content

Cultural references and societal mores are essential considerations, but more basic even than those is the need to translate content into various languages - after all, if the target audience can't even read the material, it doesn't really matter what it says. With that in mind, what is the best approach to this? Should an organization do a direct line-by-line translation of the English page, or create entirely new material for each language?

"If looking to replicate [an English site] in different languages, brands need to consider the amount of content that will need translation and how this will be achieved – choices include internal staff, external translation companies or integrated translation solutions linked to the website’s content management system (CMS)," Shawn Cabral wrote in B2BMarketing.

Utilizing a CMS can assist with other aspects of an organization's online globalization initiatives, Cabral indicated. For example, organizations that generate a significant amount of content in a variety of languages can use the CMS as a central hub for information, while also leveraging it to manage a network of sites and share content that is language-specific. One high-tech approach to localization is to utilize GPS or Geo-IP software to pinpoint users' locations and direct them to a site in their own language. Organizations can also use country domain IDs like .de or .fr for their location-specific content.

With so many different protocols for each market, managing an organization's web content and marketing messages can seem like an overwhelming task, particularly when multiple languages are involved. But by paying attention to cultural differences and using the right tools to manage their content, organizations can successfully embark on international campaigns.
