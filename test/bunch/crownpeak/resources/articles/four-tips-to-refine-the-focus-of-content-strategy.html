Four tips to refine the focus of content strategy

Developing a successful content strategy is about more than delivering a single message as clearly as possible. To fully leverage the benefits of content marketing, organizations may have to find an approach that works for them and their audience specifically, rather than following industry trends or codified marketing advice.

“Truth is, handling content is easy to do wrong,” marketing expert Guillaume Bouchard explained in a recent Search Engine Watch column. “And it’s easy to do wrong because there’s no concrete way of doing it right.”

Bouchard proposed four areas to target when refining a content strategy.

Communicating effectively

In an increasingly crowded space, audience attention is not won through flashy displays but rather by offering a clear vision that tactfully articulates its main points. Bouchard emphasized the importance of “stitching together the brand vision,” “enriching the texture of your messaging” and “focusing on the user’s course through a string of ideas.”

“One of the quickest ways to lose your prospects at the gate is to keep them guessing and frustrated about what you offer,” marketing executive Michelle Salater explained in a column for iMedia Connection.

Knowing the audience

Bouchard recommended taking steps to research potential partners and clients in a way that yields a rounded, comprehensive vision of who they are. By interviewing existing clients, organizations can learn what their most important offerings are. Web analytics tools can provide an idea of the vocabulary that site visitors are using, and site administrators can also look at the content being examined to better understand audience behavior.

Using this knowledge, marketers can better tailor content and provide information designed for smoother, more intuitive navigation.

Defining objectives

While improving content marketing messaging is one part of having an effective content strategy, it is not the only important approach, Bouchard explained. He noted that organizations may also want to have goals such as improving search or simplifying site navigation as priorities.

Just as it's important to avoid tunnel vision in setting objectives, it's crucial to adopt a wide lens when considering how content is presented on a website. Marketing expert Derek Edmond noted in a column for Search Engine Land that many marketers spend so much time worrying about the message on their homepage that they neglect the content on other parts of their site, such as the 404 page or the About page. Pointing visitors toward information can often be as important a priority as making the information itself clear.

Bouchard recommended setting definable, progress-oriented objectives for content rather than worrying about creating content that will go viral and generate a large amount of short-term attention. By setting goals that have clear, measurable criteria, organizations can then track the effectiveness of their efforts in reaching their targets.

Going beyond copy

A content strategy must do more than clearly lay out value propositions or improve SEO. To truly succeed, Bouchard suggested, content should meet the expectations of its audience, which are variable and, in many cases, not yet defined.

“Perhaps guest blogging, infographic dissemination, or viral vlogging aren’t the solutions your [organization] needs in order to excel,” Bouchard wrote. “It’s better to realize this early on, instead of being caught up in rudimentary content strategy trends. More content than ever before is being published online and, the way things are going, there’s no slowing down - it’s critical that yours remains afloat.”

For organizations trying to communicate effectively, it may be necessary to recognize that a content marketing strategy as defined by outside analysts may not be the right approach for a specific audience. Instead, organizations can take the time to determine what metrics, formats and messages work for them and their clients.
