Challenges for a digital strategy in China

Many organizations in the United States and Europe have found it difficult to expand into China, with even high profile brands struggling to compete in local markets. According to a report by the MIT Sloan Management Review, there are several common barriers to Chinese expansion, as well as certain best practices for entering the market.

Barriers to expansion

The publication’s study focused on the struggles facing major ecommerce retailers, but its findings are relevant to any organization looking to understand the unique digital challenges China presents. The study found that many organizations struggled after failing to modify their operations for a Chinese customer base.

In one example given, researchers observed the success of a Chinese auction site over an American competitor. They found the Chinese site presented a stickier, more social interface geared to local consumer interest in keeping up with trends and, as a result, retained more users. Additionally, the American site’s insistence on using a standard platform across all its operations rather than integrating its Chinese affiliate site’s platform with its own caused slower performance. An integrated solution might have allowed the site to maintain its original user-friendly performance, researchers suggested.

Regulatory pressure can also be a challenge for organizations seeking entry to the Chinese market. Local competitors may have an advantage over foreign groups due to less stringent regulation and a better presence on the ground in China. A study by IT research institute BCS suggested Western organizations consider working with a locally based hosting provider that may have already navigated the regulatory process. China’s diffuse array of networks and provincial regulations can also complicate operations in specific parts of the country. BCS recommended organizations focus on the specific geographic areas in China they hope to target. In general, the institute advocated patience in dealing with regulatory pressures.

Advice for success

The MIT Sloan Management Review advocated for flexibility across all steps of an expansion into the Chinese market, from corporate strategy to handling customer interactions. Given the tough regulatory environment, for instance, organizations may find that it is not worth their time to focus on the same product offerings as in other markets, particularly where content is concerned. The country’s stringent set of censorship practices means that organizations might benefit from targeting less censor-sensitive divisions of their operations as candidates for expansion into China.

Given the competitive environment, organizations may also want to consider partnering with a Chinese affiliate rather than creating their own channels for expansion, researchers said. By working with local partners, it is possible to leverage an existing brand - which is particularly valuable in an environment where brands carry added significance, as noted by a recent McKinsey Quarterly report.

Chinese consumers are more likely to use social networks and recommendations to shape their perception of a brand, and online discussion can easily bolster or tarnish an organization’s reputation, the Sloan Management Review said. As a result, excellent consumer-facing service and social engagement should be a part of any organization’s Chinese strategy. Rather than looking to streamline or automate every IT process, organizations should focus on consumer needs, as these are the most important factors in a brand’s success. Flexible, scalable integration should be the goal rather than perfect automation.

The Chinese market is rapidly changing and expanding across a range of industries, and any effort to globalize must be able to adapt and react to unique circumstances. The Sloan Management Review researchers, Xin Wang and Z. Justin Ren, pointed out that the biggest challenge in global strategy for any organization is striking a balance between customization and standardization appropriate to its vision.

“There is no one-size-fits-all solution,” they wrote, explaining that taking local culture into account is essential for adapting a brand to a new market
