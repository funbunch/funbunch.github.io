Win the battle against ‘zombie content’

Organizations turn to content marketing to drive brand interest and improve audience engagement, but poorly realized content can easily do the opposite. Site visitors will not stick around to investigate lifeless content, and even Google's algorithm will try to avoid it. According to a recent Search Engine Watch column by Christina Zila, organizations should seek to mitigate what she calls “zombie content.”

Zombie content, according to Zila, fails to generate attention, offers a one-note message, lacks originality or lingers in need of updates. She suggested a remedy for each of these problems.

Fails to generate attention

If content does not apply to the target audience or lacks the ability to prompt discussion, it is not serving its purpose. Zila noted that generic information on a topic or standard product descriptions are common types of such content. She suggested replacing lifeless content with material that has a more dynamic tone and a focus on audience interests.

Offers a one-note message

Content marketing often attempts to present a more complex message than a simple call-to-action or a suggestion to buy a product. Zila explained that audiences are not looking for a sales pitch, but rather a discussion of subjects that interest them and the challenges that they confront. Organizations should seek to understand client problems and address them with appropriate content, even if the solution calls for something the organization cannot provide.

A study from BtoB found that content marketers consider sales to be only the fourth most important role of the practice, behind lead generation, brand awareness and thought leadership. To achieve these other goals, content marketers need to present well-rounded content

Lacks originality

In a crowded online environment in which many sources are revisiting the same ideas and rewriting the same articles, content marketers can stand out by taking a new approach, refuting an existing claim or creating new insight by bringing ideas together, Zila wrote.

Noting that nearly 70 percent of content marketers in a recent Curata survey reported struggling with generating original content, Technorati contributor Allison Reilly suggested organizations involve more people in the content creation process to generate new insights. She also suggested organizations conduct their own research or repurpose their own content in new formats.

Lingers in need of updates

The longer content sits undisturbed, the more vulnerable it is to competitors displacing it. Zila suggested marketers update evergreen content regularly, even if the update is a simple addition such as a new headline or added chart.

Zombies may be featured in current hit TV shows and movies, but when it comes to marketing, content should not come across as "undead." Luckily, by following advice llike Zila's, marketers should find it relatively painless to breath new life into online content.
