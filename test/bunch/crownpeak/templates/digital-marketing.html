Oversee a smooth SaaS integration by focusing on data management

The proliferation of software-as-a-service (SaaS) offerings has made it possible for organizations to tap into best-of-breed solutions tailored to almost any need. To make use of these applications, however, organizations need them to be connected. As opposed to enterprise systems, which required extensive coding to make this integration happen, SaaS integration relies on sharing data from APIs between applications.

This difference means that connecting SaaS applications is a process that, above all, requires careful planning and organization-wide oversight, according to a recent Information Management article. Organizations need a strategic view to make sure all data sources are incorporated while retaining a high level of service and adhering to data governance policies. Without a comprehensive integration strategy, the organization will quickly become a tangle of point-to-point, one-off integrations that impede the effectiveness of using a wide range of different solutions.

"Cloud computing makes an enterprise-wide service integration strategy more important than ever," wrote IT support professional Chris Dancy. "Before the growth of SaaS, the need for an integration strategy was something that CIOs could put on the back burner, but the advent of cloud brings it to the forefront â€¦ CIOs and architects must make the case for an integration strategy by aligning it with business initiatives."

Dancy explained that an integration process should begin by cataloguing existing systems, determining how they communicate with each other and identifying what objects are being passed back and forth. Since the vendor has already worked to develop a well-made solution, coding is less of a concern. Instead, developing a logical data model and establishing common definitions between different applications is a priority.

Dancy recommended budgeting twice as much time for discussing how data is shared as for writing software. In organizations where data models were clearly defined before beginning the transition, major integrations have been condensed from lasting several months to just a few weeks.

Trends in SaaS integration

A recent InformationWeek article noted that SaaS applications are increasingly developing solutions and partnerships to encourage integration, with embedded analytics functions in particular demand. As data-driven decision making plays an increasingly large role in organizations' operations, users want analytics capabilities within the apps such as content management systems (CMS) that are used every day.

"The need for insight usually goes beyond the confines of a single app, so it's important to consider partner offerings and options for working with well-known BI systems and data warehousing platforms," InformationWeek's Doug Henschen wrote.

Another trend the article pointed out is a rise in configuration options in cloud software that do away with the need for customization. Rather than supplying organizations with applications that require custom coding to fit specific industries or uses - and, as a result, require testing and tweaking with every update - vendors are building applications that are more easily configured through menus of existing settings. As a result, SaaS integration is likely to become even less focused on coding and require a proportionally larger focus on planning to unify data definitions and models.
